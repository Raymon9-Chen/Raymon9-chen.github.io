Introduction¶

In recent years, Pokémon cards have seen a dramatic resurgence in popularity, driven by both nostalgic collectors and new enthusiasts. This renewed interest has led to a sharp rise in the market value of many cards, especially those of higher rarities such as Illustration Rares, Special Rares, and Hyper Rares. While rarity is a property officially designated by the card's manufacturer, price is a dynamic, crowd-driven signal that reflects not only rarity, but also demand, aesthetic appeal, and more. Yet, with the explosion of card sales online–especially through platforms like eBay and TCGPlayer–price has arguably become one of the most visible indicators of a card’s perceived value.

This increase in market activity has raised important questions for collectors, investors, and researchers alike. Among them: Can the rarity of a Pokemon card be predicted based on its price?

In this project, we aim to investigate the relationship between a card’s price and its rarity designation–specifically, we build a predictive model that uses price data as input to estimate a card’s rarity class. By examining the predictive power of price, we hope to uncover broader insights into what drives value in the Pokemon card market and how closely rarity, a theoretically static attribute, aligns with real-world consumer behavior.

Understanding this relationship has practical implications. For buyers, such a model could help detect over- or under-priced cards based on their rarity, potentially helping them make more informed purchasing decisions. For sellers, it offers a way to benchmark pricing strategies against perceived rarity. And for researchers and hobbyists, it provides a data-driven lens through which to explore the intersection of gaming culture, economics, and predictive modeling.

Data Collection¶

TCGCSV.com is a platform that compiles and updates daily CSV files containing pricing information for trading card games such as Pokémon. The dataset is structured into four levels: categories (e.g., Pokémon), groups (specific sets like SV01–SV09), products (individual cards with metadata like name, rarity, and set number), and market prices (including low, mid, and high prices). While TCGPlayer’s API doesn’t offer historical prices, TCGCSV archives daily price snapshots starting from February 8, 2024. This allows us to track trends over time if needed.

We focused on cards from the Scarlet & Violet era, filtering for the rarities most relevant to collectors and resellers. These are Illustration Rare, Special Illustration Rare, and Hyper Rare. These cards were chosen due to their higher market value and greater volatility, making them ideal for statistical analysis. We excluded lower-rarity cards because their prices were typically under one dollar with minimal fluctuation. def fill_market_price(row):
    if pd.isna(row['marketPrice']):
        low_high_average = (row['lowPrice'] + row['highPrice'])/2
        row['marketPrice'] = round(low_high_average, 2)
    return row['marketPrice']

master_df['marketPrice'] = master_df.apply(fill_market_price, axis=1)

TEST I: Test for mean card price per rarity across sets¶

Our first test determines if the mean price of cards across the same rarity vary significantly across all relevant sets (scarlet and violet main and special sets). Since we testing multiple categories and observing if they exist within the same distribution, an ANOVA test would be appropriate.

Significance level: $\alpha = 0.5$ (Level of confidence = 95%)

$H_0$: There is no significant difference in the prices of the same card rarity across the relevant sets.

$H_a$: There exists significant differences in the prices of the same card rarity across the relevant sets.

Primary Analysis¶

Based on our exploration, we chose K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) to classify card rarities based on market price. KNN is a non-parametric method that works well with small feature sets and can capture non-linear relationships. Since our feature (market price) is continuous and our target (rarity) is categorical, KNN allows us to classify by proximity to similar-priced cards. We tuned hyperparameters such as k and distance metric using cross-validation. Despite some class imbalance, KNN provided an interpretable baseline for understanding how card price relates to rarity. We also evaluated performance using accuracy and F1 scores. We found from this that KNN performed reasonably well for the more common classes but struggled with rarer ones. This highlights the limitations of using price alone and motivates further analysis with more complex models.

Insights and Conclusions¶

In this project, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of Pokémon card prices from the Scarlet & Violet series using both statistical methods and machine learning. Our exploration revealed several key insights into how card prices vary by rarity and set, and how those prices can relate to a card's popularity and market volatility.

First, our ANOVA and Tukey post-hoc tests showed statistically significant differences in card prices across sets for all three rarities. Particularly for Hyper Rare and Special Illustration Rare cards, specific sets stood out with significantly higher prices, suggesting that certain sets may drive collector and reseller interest more than others.

We also established a significant negative correlation between average set price and booster pack price ranking (used as a proxy for popularity), indicating that more popular sets may tend to have lower card prices, possibly due to higher supply and wider availability. This offers a useful insight for both sellers and collectors looking to identify undervalued or overvalued sets based on their popularity.

Our volatility analysis found no significant difference in price fluctuations across rarities, suggesting that while absolute prices vary, the degree of fluctuation in prices is relatively stable across rarities. This could indicate a consistent level of market speculation or trading activity across card tiers.

Most directly addressing our central research question, we trained a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) classifier to predict a card’s rarity based on its price. Our model achieved an accuracy of over 70%, demonstrating that price alone is a moderately strong predictor of rarity. However, the model struggled with rarer categories, emphasizing the limitations of using a single numerical feature. This insight motivates the need to explore multi-feature models, possibly incorporating metadata such as card artwork type, set popularity, print volume, or historical pricing trends for better classification performance.
